## Capex is Not Profit: The Unproven ROI of AI Infrastructure

The "Black Agent" enthusiastically points to projected annual AI infrastructure spending of $700 billion as a structural shift, guaranteeing long-term returns. However, capital expenditure, no matter how substantial, is merely an investment. It is not, in itself, a profit. Companies like Meta, Amazon, Alphabet, and Microsoft are indeed pouring vast sums into AI, but the crucial question remains unanswered: where are the *earnings*? We must differentiate between the act of "building AI" â€“ which creates a near-term drag on free cash flow and profitability â€“ and "profiting from AI" â€“ which requires a clear, measurable return on that investment. Until we see concrete evidence of enhanced profitability and positive cash flow directly attributable to these AI investments in 2H26 earnings reports, the celebration of capex is premature.

## Valuation Risks: Expectations Outpacing Reality

The market's exuberance has driven semiconductor equipment makers' valuations up an astounding 40-74% year-to-date. The Black Agent cites this as "unshakeable conviction." Yet, these gains are largely predicated on *estimates* and *expectations* of future growth, not on validated, quarter-over-quarter earnings growth proportionate to such rapid valuation expansion. This creates significant valuation risk. History is replete with examples where market enthusiasm for transformative technologies leads to irrational valuations, only to correct sharply when fundamentals fail to materialize as quickly as anticipated. Investors are paying today for profits that are still highly theoretical, exposing them to considerable downside if the narrative shifts even slightly.

## Crowding and Euphoria: Echoes of Past Bubbles

The "Black Agent's" assertion that "momentum speaks volumes" and that "fighting it is a fool's errand" is precisely the kind of sentiment that precedes market corrections. The KOSPI surging past 5400, hitting record highs, coupled with heavy domestic individual investor buying in the Korean market, are classic indicators of euphoria. When everyone is "in," and the belief is that prices can only go up, the market becomes crowded and vulnerable. Prudent investors understand that true value is found when assets are undervalued, not when they are bought on the strength of a collective, self-reinforcing narrative. Unquestioning conviction, especially when accompanied by rapid price appreciation without commensurate earnings growth, often signals peak optimism rather than sustainable value.

## The Imperative for Profit Proof: Distinguishing Hype from Health

The "Black Agent" challenges the bear case by asking for an alternative growth engine. The alternative is not to deny the potential of AI, but to demand *proof of profit* before committing capital at elevated prices. Investing should be about acquiring a share of a company's future earnings and assets, not merely participating in a popular trend. We must distinguish between the undeniable technological advancements of AI and the financial health of the companies leveraging it. Our position advocates for hedging AI exposure, allocating capital to defensive sectors or demonstrably undervalued businesses, and patiently awaiting concrete validation of a positive return on these massive AI investments. Only when the earnings truly catch up to the hype will the "AI supercycle" be fundamentally justified.
